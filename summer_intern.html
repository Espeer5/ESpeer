An Internship with Texas Department of Transportation

Developing knowledge of agile project management and how it interfaces with the ITIL framework to enable great IT work.

8/12/22

When I started looking for a Summer internship for 2022, I didn't know what I was looking for. As I work towards my goal of creating great software, I am keenly aware that programming is far from the only skill required. Before work ever lands into the hands of a developer, a huge amount of research and design work is required to gain an understanding of what needs to be created. I knew I needed to explore this; from my classwork my programming skills were progressing rapidly, but I had little to no understanding of how to maneuver within the professional environment around software. A need to explore software production at a higher level made a project management internship at Texas Department of Transportation the ideal choice for my professional internship in 2022.

What did I do?

In my internship at TxDOT, I acted as a project manager at the portfolio level and at the project level, gaining experience as both a technical project manager and at a higher level. My daily activities consisted of running project status meetings, creating requirements documents, analyzing project metrics and creating leadership reports, and building dashboards from the project portfolio database. I was involved with a variety of teams across a large IT organization, and got to forge working relationships with people who had so much to teach me.

The most unique aspect about working for TxDOT was the keen awareness that none of us were working for the bottom line; we were working for the people of the state Texas. Every project I worked on was providing benefit to a user somewhere in the state, and every coworker I worked with understood that what we did each day was part of an ongoing effort to ensure that every user of our transportation system made it home to their loved ones happy and healthy. This meant that in everything I did, my user was the priority. For example, when I designed and produced dashboards from the projects database, my user was ITD leadership, and my goal had to be giving our leaders the quickest, easiest to navigate view of the metrics so that they could quickly view the dashboard and easily understand where we were, so that they could focus on where we were going. The better my dashboard was, the quicker they could get the data they needed, and the faster they could focus on architecting a safer, better future for the state of Texas.

Through the variety of teams I was involved in, I got to experience a variety of roles. As a technical PM, I facilitated meetings and directly baselined and implemented agile and hybrid project schedules. As a team lead on an intern project, I set the direction, coordinated with other teams, and led workshops within my team to develop ideas into designs. As a reporter and dashboard developer, I received requirements documents and created templates and demos for review, received commentary, and put prototypes into production. No matter which hat I was wearing on a particular team, I learned how to maneuver and communicate within an ITIL driven environment effectively. This breadth of experience was the best part of working at TxDOT, where I worked every day on real problems and got to make a real impact on real products.
